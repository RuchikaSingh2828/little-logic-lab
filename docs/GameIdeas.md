## Activities

### Picture Sudoku 🟢

**Ages:** 3–4 | **Skills:** Pattern recognition, row/column logic

Children solve a grid puzzle using picture cards instead of numbers. Each row and column must contain one of each image — like a printable preschool worksheet with cut-out pieces. Drag picture cards from a tray into empty cells, or tap to place.

**MVP interaction:** Drag-and-drop emoji cards into a 2×2 or 3×3 grid; calm feedback on correct placement; no scores or timers.

---

### Find the Missing Piece ⚪

**Ages:** 3–5 | **Skills:** Visual closure, part-whole reasoning

A simple scene or pattern is shown with one piece missing. The child picks the correct piece from a small set of options to complete the picture. Builds the ability to mentally fill in gaps and see how parts fit into a whole.

**MVP interaction:** Tap one of 3–4 picture options to fill the blank spot in a scene.

---

### Complete the Sequence ⚪

**Ages:** 3–6 | **Skills:** Ordering, prediction, cause-effect

A row of images follows a logical order (size, color, alternating pattern, daily routine). One or more items are missing. The child chooses what comes next or fills the gap. Develops the habit of looking for rules in ordered things.

**MVP interaction:** Drag the correct card into the empty slot at the end or middle of a sequence.

---

### Spot the Difference ⚪

**Ages:** 4–7 | **Skills:** Attention to detail, visual discrimination

Two nearly identical illustrations are shown side by side. The child taps the items that differ between them. Encourages careful looking rather than rushing — a calm concentration exercise.

**MVP interaction:** Tap differences on the right image; gentle confirmation per find; no countdown.

---

### Which Doesn't Belong? ⚪

**Ages:** 3–6 | **Skills:** Classification, categorical thinking

A group of 3–5 pictures is shown where one item doesn't fit the category (e.g., three fruits and one vehicle). The child taps the odd one out. Introduces the idea that things can be grouped by shared attributes.

**MVP interaction:** Tap the item that doesn't belong; brief calm explanation of why.

*Also known as: Odd One Out*

---

### Build the Pattern ⚪

**Ages:** 3–6 | **Skills:** Pattern extension, repetition rules

A repeating pattern is started (AB, AAB, or color/shape sequences) and the child continues it by placing the next pieces. More open-ended than Complete the Sequence — the child extends rather than fills a single gap.

**MVP interaction:** Drag cards into empty slots to extend a pattern row across the screen.

---

### Logic Stories ⚪

**Ages:** 5–7 | **Skills:** Narrative reasoning, if-then thinking

A short illustrated story presents a simple problem ("The bunny wants the carrot but the path is blocked"). The child makes 1–2 choices that follow logical cause and effect. No wrong-ending punishment — gentle redirect if the choice doesn't solve the problem.

**MVP interaction:** Tap choice cards at story decision points; see the outcome illustrated.

---

### Shadow Matching ⚪

**Ages:** 3–5 | **Skills:** Shape recognition, visual-spatial reasoning

Objects are shown alongside their shadows (or silhouettes). The child matches each object to its correct shadow. Builds shape constancy — recognizing a form regardless of fill or color.

**MVP interaction:** Drag objects onto matching shadow outlines.

---

### Color Sorting ⚪

**Ages:** 3–4 | **Skills:** Classification, attribute sorting

A collection of mixed-color objects must be sorted into color groups. Large buckets or zones on screen receive dragged items. Simple, satisfying, and very approachable for the youngest age band.

**MVP interaction:** Drag colored picture cards into labeled color zones.

---

### Size Ordering ⚪

**Ages:** 3–5 | **Skills:** Seriation, comparative reasoning

Three to five items of the same object at different sizes must be arranged from smallest to largest (or largest to smallest). Introduces comparative language and ordering by a continuous attribute.

**MVP interaction:** Drag cards into ordered slots on a horizontal line.

---

### Animal Classification ⚪

**Ages:** 4–6 | **Skills:** Grouping, taxonomy basics

Cards showing animals must be sorted into groups (farm, ocean, forest; or mammals, birds, fish). More conceptual than color sorting — the child must understand category membership.

**MVP interaction:** Drag animal cards into 2–3 labeled group areas.

---

### Maze Escape ⚪

**Ages:** 4–7 | **Skills:** Planning, spatial reasoning

A simple top-down maze with wide paths and a clear start and finish. The child traces or taps a path for a character to follow. No timer; dead ends simply stop the character with a gentle "let's try another way."

**MVP interaction:** Swipe or tap to move character one step at a time through grid maze.

---

### Treasure Hunt Logic ⚪

**Ages:** 5–7 | **Skills:** Multi-step deduction, map reading

A simple map with 2–3 clues ("The treasure is not near water" / "It's in a place with trees"). The child eliminates locations and picks the remaining spot. Introduces deductive reasoning in a playful narrative frame.

**MVP interaction:** Tap locations on a map to rule them out; tap final answer when sure.

---

### Memory Flip Cards ⚪

**Ages:** 3–6 | **Skills:** Working memory, concentration

A small grid of face-down cards (4–12 pairs). The child flips two at a time to find matching pairs. Classic memory game with calm design — no speed pressure, no combo multipliers.

**MVP interaction:** Tap to flip cards; matched pairs stay face-up; session ends when all pairs found.

---

### Tangram Puzzles ⚪

**Ages:** 5–7 | **Skills:** Spatial composition, geometry intuition

Flat geometric pieces are dragged and rotated to fill a silhouette outline (cat, house, boat). Builds intuition for how shapes combine to form larger forms.

**MVP interaction:** Drag and rotate tangram pieces into a ghost outline on a board.

---

### Coding Without Code ⚪

**Ages:** 5–7 | **Skills:** Sequencing, algorithmic thinking

A character needs to reach a goal. The child builds a sequence of action blocks (move forward, turn left, pick up) and presses "run" to watch the character follow the program. Introduces programming concepts without syntax.

**MVP interaction:** Drag action blocks into a sequence strip; tap Run to animate.

## From README — Additional Ideas

### Pattern Completion ⚪

**Ages:** 3–5 | **Skills:** Pattern recognition, visual prediction

Similar to Build the Pattern but focused on a single missing element in an established visual pattern (shapes, colors, alternating designs). The child identifies and places the one piece that completes the design.

**MVP interaction:** Tap or drag the correct tile into one empty cell in a pattern grid.

---

### Shape Matching ⚪

**Ages:** 3–5 | **Skills:** Shape recognition, visual matching

Outline shapes or silhouettes are shown alongside a set of filled shapes. The child matches each shape to its pair. A simpler, more direct version of Shadow Matching focused on geometric forms.

**MVP interaction:** Drag filled shapes onto matching outlines.

---

### Sorting & Classification ⚪

**Ages:** 3–6 | **Skills:** Categorization, logical grouping

An umbrella activity combining Color Sorting, Size Ordering, and Animal Classification into mixed challenges. Objects vary by one or more attributes and the child sorts them into the correct groups.

**MVP interaction:** Drag items into 2–4 labeled bins based on displayed category rules.

---

### Number & Logic Games ⚪

**Ages:** 6–7 | **Skills:** Numeracy, logical reasoning

A collection of number-based activities for the oldest age band: counting sequences, simple addition puzzles, number sudoku (4×4, 6×6, 9×9), and comparison games ("which group has more?"). Bridges toward formal math while keeping play-based interaction.

**MVP interaction:** Varies by sub-game; start with 4×4 number sudoku and counting sequences.

---

## Design Principles (All Activities)

- No streaks, coins, XP, leaderboards, or daily rewards
- No timers that create stress
- Calm, educational feedback ("Nice observation." not "AWESOME!")
- Short sessions (5–10 minutes)
- Healthy stopping points — never pressure to continue
- Montessori feel: large touch targets, soft colors, minimal animation
